Replication Report on Renshon (2014)

Sean Hughes

Abstract

I replicate and expand upon the work in “Physiological Arousal and Political Beliefs” (CITATION) using data and replication code from the Harvard Dataverse (CITATION). In this paper Renshon, Lee, and Tingley explore the effects of incidental emotions on political beliefs. Specifically, by showing that heightened physiological reactivity, measured by SCL, mediated the relationship between anxiety and anti-immigration attitude, they are able to provide evidence that emotions incidental to the decision process can have important effects on our political beliefs.

I replicate 2 figures and 1 table from the article (located in the Appendix below). I also test the results of the study using alternative modeling techniques.

Extension Analysis

I will further explore the studies findings regarding the Effects of Physiological Arousal on Immigration Preferences. Specifically, this means comparing the significance of treatment vs. the responses to the immigration questionare. We take these variables and run a series of models to verify the robustness of the results.

Basic Linear Model

Using the main results table (Appendix Table 1) the authors claim that skin conductance reactivity is a significant predictor of immigration preference. Note that the authors of the paper choose to filter out data from the “RELAX” condition, citing that the results remain unchanged if included. We will include the RELAX condition and combine the 3 treatments into a binary variable with the following values: - 1 if treatment condition is Anxiety - 0 if treatment condition is Neutral or Relax The authors claim that treatment is a significant predictor of SCL. They also claim that skin conductance reactivity is a significant predictor of immigration preference. While these claims together might suggest that treatment condition was a significant predictor of the immigration DV, we can see below that it is not.
